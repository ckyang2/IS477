**Overview:**  
This project investigates the impact of essential fiscal factors on the growth of U.S. federal debt over time. The analysis examines the connections among government expenditure, tax income, interest rates, and the overall sum of national debt still outstanding in the market and doesn’t account for paid off debt as it no longer pays interest. The project seeks to identify trends and possible correlations among these variables by merging datasets from various government sources and connecting them through common time attributes like year or quarter in order to maintain the time value of money and display the power of compounding over time.  
Our main research question examines how variations in government expenditure, tax income, and interest rates affect the growth or reduction of federal debt with supporting questions examine these connections both separately and together, such as how expenditure influences debt growth, how tax income affects budget shortfalls or paying down debt, and how interest rates shape the expense of managing current debt.  
By integrating data, conducting exploratory analysis, and creating visualizations, the project aims to gain deeper insights into the financial dynamics that influence long-term shifts in U.S. federal debt and hopefully explain the causation along with potential solutions if casual relationships exist.

**Research or business question:**  
The goal of this project is to analyze the relationship between government fiscal activity and the growth of U.S. federal debt.

Primary Question:  
How do changes in U.S. government spending, tax revenue, and interest rates influence the growth of outstanding federal debt over time?

Supporting Questions:  
1\. What relationship exists between federal spending and increases in outstanding national debt?  
2\. How does tax revenue affect budget deficits and debt growth?  
3\. How do interest rate changes impact government interest payments and debt sustainability?  
4\. To what extent do spending, revenue, and interest rates together explain long-term trends in U.S. federal debt?

**Datasets:**  
[https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/FEDFUNDS](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/FEDFUNDS)  
[https://fiscaldata.treasury.gov/datasets/historical-debt-outstanding/historical-debt-outstanding](https://fiscaldata.treasury.gov/datasets/historical-debt-outstanding/historical-debt-outstanding)  
	The first dataset is the historical debt outstanding, it provides an annual summary of the total U.S. federal debt outstanding from 1789 to the present. We noticed that the fiscal calendar changed over time with the fiscal year beginning in January from 1789–1842, July from 1842–1977, and October from 1977 to the present which might require a change later when cleaning. We also noticed that it does not include a detailed breakdown of debt components so we might need other data sets to link to this data for additional information. The second dataset is the federal funds effective rate and it measures the interest rate at which U.S. depository institutions lend reserve balances to one another overnight. Since the federal funds rate strongly influences broader market interest rates, including loans and other financial instruments, it will provide important context for understanding the cost of servicing government debt. Starting with the two datasets along with other data that we will include later on the project by linking over a common time factor, we can use them to analyze trends and relationships between monetary policy and debt growth.

**Constraints**  
Several limitations may affect the analysis in this project. First, the datasets used come from different government sources and may vary in format, reporting frequency like monthly, quarterly, or yearly, and definitions of fiscal metrics. This may require additional data cleaning and transformation before the datasets can be integrated along with missing or contradictory data based on different calculation methods and stories they want to put forth. Second, the analysis will primarily rely on historical aggregated data, which may limit the ability to capture short term policy effects, black swan events, or causal relationships between variables such as spending, revenue, interest rates, and debt. Differences in how datasets report time periods may also introduce challenges when aligning records for accurate comparison. Black swan events like the great depression and the dot com bubble may inflate and create major up and down trends that can’t be easily compared with other historical periods. Finally, while the datasets can show correlations between fiscal variables, they may not fully explain all factors influencing federal debt growth, such as economic shocks, policy changes, or external events as the market can’t be described simply with few economic factors in this project. As a result, conclusions drawn from the analysis should be interpreted as indicative relationships rather than definitive causal explanations and be treated accordingly.
